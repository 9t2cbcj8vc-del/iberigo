Opening a Bank Account in Spain

A bank account makes everyday life easier in Spain. It can help with rent, salary, utilities, local payments and tax admin, but the right account depends on whether you are already resident and what documents you have.

Start practical

You do not always need the perfect long-term bank on day one. Some people start with a flexible online option while preparing local documents.

Quick Answer

Most people moving to Spain need a bank account for rent, salary, utilities and daily payments. Banks usually need to identify you and may ask for passport or ID, NIE or TIE if you have one, address evidence, income evidence and your tax-residence information. Exact requirements vary by bank and by account type.

Before You Start

Know why you need it

Salary, rent, utilities, savings, self-employment and daily spending can point to different account needs.

Check your document stage

Your options may differ if you already have NIE, padrón, EU registration, TIE or proof of income.

Compare fees

Look at monthly fees, card fees, transfer costs, ATM access and minimum conditions before opening.

Tip

If you are still waiting for Spanish paperwork, ask the bank whether it has a non-resident, newcomer or passport-based opening route.

Official Requirements

Identity checks

Banks must identify customers before opening accounts. Expect passport, EU national ID, NIE, TIE or equivalent identity evidence depending on your situation.

Customer profile

Banks may ask where you live, where your money comes from, whether you are tax resident, and how you plan to use the account.

Account type

Resident, non-resident and online accounts can have different document requests and limits.

Important

Do not assume one bank’s answer applies everywhere. If one bank cannot open the account yet, another may have a different onboarding route.

Documents you'll usually prepare

Common bank onboarding documents

  • Passport or EU national identity card.
  • NIE, TIE or EU registration certificate if you already have one.
  • Address evidence such as padrón, rental contract, utility bill or foreign address proof.
  • Income evidence such as work contract, payslip, pension statement, tax return or savings evidence.
  • Tax-residence information and foreign tax number if the bank asks for it.
  • Spanish phone number or email for app and security verification.
Document reality

Banks can ask for different evidence depending on your nationality, residency status, income source and account type.

Practical Advice

Ask about Spanish IBAN

A Spanish IBAN can make rent, utilities, local payroll and some direct debits easier.

Keep a backup

Do not rely on one card during your first weeks. Keep a second card or account available.

Check language support

If you are not comfortable in Spanish yet, ask about English support in branch, app and customer service.

Tip

Before choosing, ask the bank to show the full fee schedule, not only the headline account name.

Step-by-Step Process

  1. 1

    Decide what the account is for

    Salary, rent, daily spending, self-employment or savings may point to different choices.

  2. 2

    Prepare identity and address evidence

    Gather passport or ID, NIE/TIE if available, address evidence and income evidence.

  3. 3

    Compare account types

    Look at resident, non-resident and online options. Compare fees and limits.

  4. 4

    Open the account

    Complete the bank’s onboarding process and keep copies of documents you submit.

  5. 5

    Test payments

    Check card activation, bank transfers, direct debits, app access and security codes before relying on it.

Common Mistakes

1

Assuming every landlord or employer accepts any European IBAN without friction.

2

Opening the first account offered without checking fees.

3

Not keeping proof of income or funds ready.

4

Relying on one card during the first weeks after arrival.

5

Ignoring tax-residence questions during bank onboarding.

Real Questions People Ask

Do I need a Spanish bank account immediately?

Not always, but it usually makes rent, utilities, payroll and local payments easier.

Can I open an account before I have a NIE?

Some banks may offer non-resident or newcomer options, but requirements vary. Ask the bank directly.

Are Revolut, bunq or Wise enough?

They can be useful starter options. For long-term life in Spain, you may still want a traditional Spanish bank account depending on rent, payroll and local direct debits.

Will the bank ask about taxes?

Often yes. Banks commonly ask about tax residence and customer profile during onboarding.
