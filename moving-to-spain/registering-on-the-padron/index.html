How to Register on the Padrón in Spain

The padrón is Spain’s local town hall address register. This guide explains what it is, who usually registers, what documents are commonly requested, and how the process normally works.

Local rules matter

The padrón is managed by each municipality. Requirements and appointment systems can vary from one town hall to another.

Quick Answer

The padrón is municipal registration with the town hall where you live. It records your address in that municipality. It is useful for many practical steps in Spain, but it is not the same as getting residence permission.

At a Glance

What it isTown hall address registration.
Where you do itAt the town hall for the municipality where you live.
Does it prove residency?No. It records your local address; it does not grant immigration status.
Do rules vary?Yes. Document lists and appointment systems can vary between municipalities.
Typical useAddress evidence for local services, healthcare, schooling, some residence processes and everyday administration.

Before You Start

Find your municipality

Use the town hall that covers the address where you actually live.

Check local rules

Do not assume another city’s document list applies to you.

Prepare address evidence

Your town hall will normally want to see why you can register at that address.

Tip

Before signing a rental contract, ask whether you can use the address for padrón registration if you need it.

What is the Padrón?

Municipal register

The padrón, formally the padrón municipal, is the local register of people living in a municipality.

Address record

It connects you to an address in a town or city. It is not an immigration permit.

Local administration

Town halls use it for planning and local services. Other offices may ask for a certificate or volante as address evidence.

Important

Registering on the padrón is different from obtaining residency. It does not give you the right to live or work in Spain by itself.

Who should register?

People living in Spain

If you are living in a Spanish municipality, padrón registration is often one of the first local admin steps.

People preparing other processes

You may need address evidence for healthcare, EU registration, school, social services or other administration.

People who move address

If you move to a new municipality, you may need to register at the new address.

Who usually cannot register?

People without a real local address

You normally need to register where you actually live.

People without acceptable address evidence

If you cannot show the town hall why you can use the address, the process may be blocked.

People using someone else’s address without permission

Many town halls require the owner, tenant or host to authorize registration if the home is not in your name.

Important

Local practices differ. If your housing situation is unusual, check directly with the town hall before assuming you can register.

Why is the Padrón important?

It proves where you live locally

Many offices use the padrón certificate or volante as address evidence.

It can unlock next steps

Healthcare, EU registration, school registration and some local services may ask for it.

It keeps your local record current

If you move, the new municipality may need your updated registration.

Documents you'll usually need

Common documents to prepare

  • Passport, EU national ID, NIE or TIE if you have one.
  • Rental contract, property deed, authorization from the owner or tenant, or other accepted address evidence.
  • Completed town hall form if your municipality provides one.
  • Appointment confirmation if the town hall uses appointments.
  • Copies of documents if the town hall requests them.
Local variation

This is a general preparation list. Town halls can ask for different documents depending on the municipality and your housing situation.

Step-by-Step Process

  1. 1

    Identify the correct town hall

    Use the municipality where your home is located.

  2. 2

    Check the local appointment system

    Some town halls use online appointments. Others allow in-person or local office filing.

  3. 3

    Prepare identity and address evidence

    Bring the documents your municipality asks for, plus copies if requested.

  4. 4

    Attend the appointment or submit locally

    The town hall checks your identity and whether the address evidence is acceptable.

  5. 5

    Request proof of registration

    Ask whether you receive a certificado de empadronamiento, volante, or another confirmation.

  6. 6

    Keep it for next steps

    Save the document with your Spain paperwork. Other processes may ask for a recent version.

Common Mistakes

1

Assuming all town halls ask for identical documents.

2

Signing housing without checking whether padrón registration is possible.

3

Confusing padrón registration with immigration residence permission.

4

Waiting until another appointment is blocked by missing address evidence.

5

Bringing only digital copies when paper copies or originals are requested.

Real Questions People Ask

Is the padrón the same as residency?

No. The padrón records your local address. Residency or residence registration is a separate process.

Do all town halls ask for the same documents?

No. Requirements can vary between municipalities and housing situations.

Can I register if I live with someone else?

Often you need permission or evidence from the owner, tenant or host. The exact document can vary locally.

What document do I get after registering?

Town halls may issue or allow you to request a certificate or volante showing your registration. Local wording can vary.

Do I need this before EU registration?

It is commonly useful and may be requested. Check the appointment instructions for your exact province and procedure.

Can I use a hotel or short stay address?

This depends on local rules and your actual housing situation. Ask the town hall before relying on it.

What Happens Next?

After registering on the padrón, keep the proof safe. You may need it for EU registration, healthcare, banking, school registration, digital access or other local administration.

Tip

If another office asks for a recent padrón certificate, check whether your town hall lets you request an updated copy online or in person.
