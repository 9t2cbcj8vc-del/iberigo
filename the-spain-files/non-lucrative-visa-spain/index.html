Non-Lucrative Visa in Spain

Published July 2026 · Checked against official sources in July 2026

In short

Spain's non-lucrative route is a temporary residence option for eligible non-EU, non-EEA and non-Swiss nationals who can support themselves without working or carrying out professional activity. The initial application is normally made through the Spanish consular office responsible for where you live.

It is not a property-purchase visa and it is not presented by the official rules as a remote-work route.

What the non-lucrative visa is

The visa is the entry stage of an initial non-lucrative temporary residence authorisation. If it is approved and you enter Spain within the visa's validity, the initial residence authorisation normally runs for one year from your date of entry.

The route is built around two core ideas: you have enough resources to live in Spain without earning from work, and you have suitable health cover. It is mainly relevant to people outside the EU free-movement system.

Who it may suit

It may be worth exploring if you want to live in Spain and your living costs can be covered by savings, pensions, investments or other resources that do not require you to work or provide professional services.

It may also be possible to apply with accompanying family members. The financial requirement rises for each family member, and the consulate will ask for evidence of the family relationship and the other documents applicable to each person.

Who should consider another route

  • If you plan to work for a Spanish employer, look at the appropriate work-authorisation route.
  • If you plan to work for yourself, look at the self-employed route.
  • If you plan to work remotely from Spain, check the current international teleworker or other work-authorised route rather than assuming non-lucrative residence permits it.
  • If you are an EU, EEA or Swiss citizen, or covered by the EU-family regime, different residence rules normally apply.
The no-work condition matters

The official definition says this residence authorisation is for living in Spain without carrying out labour or professional activities. Official sources reviewed here do not establish that remote work is permitted under this route.

If your plan depends on employment, freelancing, managing an active business or remote professional work, get route-specific advice before applying.

Financial means

The national rule uses the IPREM public-income benchmark rather than a permanently fixed euro figure. For the initial application, the main applicant must show resources equivalent to 400% of the monthly IPREM for the period requested. The benchmark increases by 100% of monthly IPREM for each accompanying family member.

IPREM and consular calculation instructions can change. Check the responsible consulate's current checklist and calculation period before relying on any euro amount. Property ownership alone does not replace the requirement to prove sufficient means.

Health insurance

The national requirement is public or private health insurance arranged with an insurer authorised to operate in Spain. Consulates may specify how the policy and certificate must be presented, so compare the policy wording with the checklist for your consular district before buying it.

Travel insurance should not be assumed to satisfy a residence-health-insurance requirement. Check coverage, exclusions, waiting periods and proof of payment with the insurer and consulate.

Common documents

The exact checklist is consulate-specific, but the national procedure includes:

  • The relevant visa application and residence-authorisation forms, including EX-01 where required
  • A complete, valid passport meeting the current validity requirements
  • Evidence of sufficient financial means for the requested period
  • Evidence of qualifying health insurance
  • Criminal-record certificates covering the countries required by the current rules
  • A medical certificate addressing the public-health requirement
  • Family-relationship documents for accompanying relatives
  • Applicable visa and residence-processing fees

Consulates can require originals, copies, particular certificate wording, recent issue dates and specific appointment or external-provider steps. Use the checklist for the consulate with jurisdiction over your place of residence.

Translation, apostille and legalisation

Foreign documents may need translation into Spanish by a sworn translator. Foreign public documents generally also need legalisation, unless an apostille or an applicable international agreement removes that requirement.

Check each document separately. A bank letter, police certificate, marriage certificate and medical certificate may not all follow the same authentication path.

Where and how to apply

The national procedure directs the initial application to the Spanish consular office responsible for the district where the applicant lives, or to an authorised external service provider or other designated mission where applicable. It is not described as a general in-Spain application route.

  1. Find the responsible Spanish consulate and its current non-lucrative visa checklist.
  2. Confirm its appointment, form, fee, translation and document-age rules.
  3. Prepare the full application for every accompanying applicant.
  4. Submit through the channel specified by that consulate.
  5. Wait for the formal decision before making irreversible moving plans.

What happens after approval

The official national guidance sets deadlines for collecting the visa and entering Spain. Follow the dates on your decision and visa rather than relying on a generic timeline.

After entry, the holder must apply personally for the TIE foreigner identity card within the official period, currently one month from entry for the initial route. This is a separate police/card step after the visa and residence authorisation.

Bringing family members

The official initial-residence guidance allows the applicant and family members to apply from their country of origin. Each accompanying person needs their own application evidence, and the main applicant must show the additional financial benchmark.

Family categories and proof requirements should be checked with the consulate. Do not assume that an informal relationship or financial dependence will be accepted without the documents the current rules require.

Renewal and longer-term planning

Renewal is a new assessment, not an automatic extension. Current national guidance requires continued sufficient means and health insurance. It also states that the holder must have lived genuinely and effectively in Spain for more than 183 days during the calendar year.

The renewed authorisation is generally valid for two years unless the person qualifies for long-term residence instead. Renewal timing, absences and the evidence needed should be reviewed well before the initial card expires.

Residence and tax are separate questions

Immigration residence, renewal residence requirements and tax residence are governed by different rules. Spending substantial time in Spain may have tax consequences, but this article does not determine anyone's tax residence.

Get qualified cross-border tax advice for your own dates, income, assets and treaty position.

Common mistakes

  • Treating a property purchase as proof that the visa will be granted
  • Assuming remote work is automatically compatible with the no-work condition
  • Using an old IPREM figure or another consulate's currency conversion
  • Buying insurance before checking the responsible consulate's wording
  • Missing apostille, legalisation or sworn-translation requirements
  • Applying through the wrong consular district
  • Planning around an unofficial guaranteed timeline
  • Forgetting the TIE step after entering Spain

Real questions people ask

Can I work remotely on this visa?

The official sources reviewed here define the route around residence without labour or professional activity. They do not provide a clear basis for telling readers that remote work is permitted. If remote work is central to your plan, check a work-authorised route.

Does buying a home qualify me?

No. Owning a home may form part of your wider evidence, but it does not replace the route's financial, insurance, criminal-record, health and procedural requirements.

Can I apply after arriving in Spain as a visitor?

The standard initial procedure is consular and is tied to the applicant's place of residence. Do not assume a tourist stay can be converted inside Spain without a separately applicable legal route.

How much money do I need?

The national benchmark is 400% of monthly IPREM for the main applicant plus 100% for each accompanying family member, calculated for the relevant period. Check the current IPREM and your consulate's instructions when you are ready to apply.

Can family members come with me?

Potentially, yes, subject to the eligible relationship, documentation and additional financial means required for each person. Confirm the exact family checklist with the responsible consulate.
